🌊 Citizen Science · Korea · China · Japan

Korea's coasts are getting
wrecked by jellyfish.
I'm trying to do something about it.

I'm a high school student in Seoul, and I've been obsessed with jellyfish blooms for the past two years. Not because they're cool (they are), but because they're quietly destroying fishing livelihoods and coastal ecosystems across three countries — and nobody seems to have a shared plan.

I started tracking blooms after reading about fishers in South Chungnam losing entire hauls to Nomura's jellyfish. Turns out the same species originates in Chinese waters, hits Korea's west coast, then reaches Japan. Three countries, one problem, zero shared database. So I built one.

Why it matters

A billion-dollar crisis hiding in plain sight

Jellyfish populations in the Yellow Sea, East China Sea, and Sea of Japan have expanded dramatically since the 1990s — with cascading damage most people never see.

Not sure if this is real? Read the science first. Award-winning research paper + competition pitch — then decide if you want to help.
See the research →
🎣

Fisheries collapse

Dense blooms rip nets, contaminate catch, and eat the same plankton as fish larvae. Fishers in Chungnam, Liaoning, and Fukui report it gets worse every summer.

Severe
⚡

Power plant shutdowns

Nuclear and thermal plants pull seawater for cooling. Jellyfish clog the intakes. This has happened in Korea, China, and Japan — not once, repeatedly.

Severe
🐠

Ecosystem tipping points

Once jellyfish eat enough fish eggs and larvae, fish populations can't recover. The ecosystem flips into a jellyfish-dominant state — possibly already happening in the Yellow Sea.

Severe
🏖️

Beach and tourism losses

Mass strandings and stings close beaches across Korea and Japan every summer. Local businesses lose income. Nobody tracks this systematically across all three countries.

Significant
♻️

But they're useful

Dried jellyfish biomass is nitrogen-rich and converts to agricultural fertilizer. Some Korean companies are already doing this. If we map the blooms, we can harvest them before they cause damage.

Opportunity
🌐

Three countries, no shared data

Korea, China, and Japan each monitor separately. A bloom starting in Qingdao becomes Korea's problem in two weeks — but nobody gets an early warning.

How citizen science works here

You don't need to be a marine biologist

Professional monitoring covers maybe 5% of the relevant coastline. The rest needs people who actually live there.

Step 01

Spot it, photo it

Near the coast and see a bloom? Take a photo and submit a report. Species, density, location, date.

Step 02

We verify it

Submissions are reviewed and cross-referenced. Verified sightings go into the shared KR-CN-JP database within 48 hours.

Step 03

Data becomes warnings

Enough data points build a picture of where blooms are moving. Fishers and beach managers get early alerts before a bloom arrives.

Step 04

Blooms get harvested

High-density alerts go to jellyfish-to-fertilizer operations. The problem funds its own solution — in theory. Read the research →

Tri-country network

One sea. Three countries. Currently zero coordination.

The Yellow Sea doesn't have a border. The jellyfish don't either. But the data does.

🇰🇷

Korea (한국)

Korea's west coast gets hit hardest by Nomura's blooms drifting in from Chinese waters. Nearshore community reporting is almost nonexistent. This is where I'm starting.

Focus: Nemopilema nomurai · Aurelia aurita
🇨🇳

China (中国)

The Yellow Sea and Bohai Bay are where Nomura's jellyfish breed before drifting east. Shandong and Liaoning fishing communities face significant annual losses.

Focus: Nemopilema nomurai · Cyanea nozakii
🇯🇵

Japan (日本)

Japan has the most sophisticated monitoring infrastructure but doesn't share data with Korea or China in real time. Connecting the networks could extend early warning times from days to weeks.

Focus: Nemopilema nomurai · Pelagia noctiluca
