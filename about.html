About Ethan & JellyWatch

I'm Ethan Yang, a student at Fayston Preparatory in Seoul. I started JellyWatch because I kept reading about jellyfish destroying Korea's west coast fisheries and couldn't find a single place that tracked it systematically across the three countries that share the problem.

The jellyfish-fertilizer angle came later. I was looking into what happens to the biomass after mass strandings and found that Korean companies were already experimenting with jellyfish-derived fertilizer at small scale — but nobody had connected the bloom-tracking problem to the harvesting opportunity. That became my research focus and eventually my Blue Ocean Competition pitch.

What I'm trying to do with JellyWatch: build the first shared, open database of jellyfish bloom sightings across Korea, China, and Japan. Professional monitoring networks cover maybe 5% of the relevant coastline. Citizen science is the only realistic way to fill the gaps.

I'm one person. I'm building this between classes. If you're a researcher, fisher, marine biologist, or just someone who walks on the coast and sees jellyfish — I want your data and I want to hear from you.

How this started

2024

First research paper

Started with a literature review: can jellyfish biomass actually work as fertilizer? The answer was yes, with caveats. The NEXST paper came out of that question.

2025

JellyFix pitch — Blue Ocean Competition

Took the research and turned it into a business concept: harvest invasive blooms, convert to high-retention fertilizer pellets, sell to drought-affected farms. Top 500 globally.

2026

JellyWatch goes live

Built the citizen science platform to address the data gap: no shared bloom database exists across KR, CN, and JP. This is the attempt to build one from the ground up.

Next

Field verification + regional coordinators

Getting volunteer coordinators in Busan, Qingdao, and western Japan. Starting field verification of bloom reports. Connecting with Korean fisheries organizations.
