Research & Awards

I've been working on the jellyfish-to-fertilizer problem since 2024 — first as a research question, then as a business concept. Here's the paper trail.

🏆 Top 500 · Blue Ocean Competition 2026

JellyFix — Turning Jellyfish Blooms into Bio-Organic Fertilizer

A business pitch and feasibility framework for harvesting invasive jellyfish and processing them into high-retention fertilizer pellets. The core insight: jellyfish collagen structure increases soil moisture retention by approximately 30% compared to conventional organic fertilizer — meaning the product isn't just cheaper than synthetic alternatives, it outperforms them in drought conditions. Placed Top 500 globally at the Blue Ocean Competition 2026.

🔬 Award Winner · NEXST Research Competition

"From Nuisance to Nutrient" — Biochemical Feasibility of Jellyfish-Derived Fertilizers for Sustainable Agriculture

A systematic literature review examining whether jellyfish biomass can function as viable organic fertilizer. The research evaluates the biochemical pathways that control nutrient release — protein hydrolysis, nitrogen mineralization, and phosphorus release — alongside salinity constraints that limit marine biomass use in agriculture. Synthesizes peer-reviewed research across marine ecology, soil chemistry, and agricultural science. Won at the NEXST Research Competition.

nitrogen mineralization protein hydrolysis marine ecology soil chemistry salinity stress sustainable agriculture
Key finding: Jellyfish tissues contain relatively labile organic compounds with a narrow carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, suggesting faster nutrient release than plant-based residues. The main constraint is salinity — but standard composting and washing techniques used in seaweed fertilizer production can address this.
